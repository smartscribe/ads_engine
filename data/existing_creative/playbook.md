# JotPsych Creative Playbook

## Executive Summary

JotPsych's Meta ads show clear winners: UGC-style photography with empathetic question hooks ($17-38 CPConv) and pain point messaging with warm earth tones ($41-64 CPConv) dramatically outperform all other approaches. Education-focused, text-heavy ads consistently fail despite $4000+ in spend, while playful tones with provocative claims generate zero conversions. The data strongly suggests clinicians respond to authentic, empathetic content that acknowledges their struggles rather than content that educates or entertains them. Screen captures of product UI perform reliably ($26-45 CPConv) when paired with clinical tone, making them a safe scaling option alongside the top-performing UGC patterns.

## Top 5 Performing Patterns

### 1. UGC-Style Video with Empathetic Question Hooks
**Average CPConv:** $17.58 - $38.62

**Taxonomy Combination:**
- Message: Pain point or value proposition
- Hook: Question
- CTA: Learn more
- Tone: Empathetic
- Visual: Photography (UGC style)
- Subject: Clinician at work
- Color: Warm earth
- Density: Headline + subhead

**Example Ads:**
- Scale: KM UGC - Video Concept 2 ($17.58, 4 conv)
- Test: KM UGC - Video Concept 2 ($31.25, 13 conv)
- Test: KM UGC - Video Concept 1 ($38.62, 26 conv)

**Why It Works:**
The UGC format creates authenticity and trust, making clinicians feel like they're hearing from a peer rather than being marketed to. Question hooks ("Are you spending hours on documentation?") engage the audience immediately by making them self-reflect, while the empathetic tone validates their struggles. The warm earth color palette feels approachable rather than corporate, and the clinician-at-work subject matter creates immediate identification with the target audience.

## Bottom 5 Performing Patterns

### 1. Playful Tone with Provocative Claims
**Average CPConv:** $0 (no conversions despite $40+ spend)

**Taxonomy Combination:**
- Message: Value proposition or social proof
- Hook: Provocative claim
- CTA: Learn more
- Tone: Playful
- Visual: Illustration or text-heavy
- Subject: Conceptual
- Color: Warm earth or bold saturated
- Density: Headline only

**Example Ads:**
- 20240415_JotPsych_SuspiciouslyGood_v03 ($16.18, 0 conv)
- 20240415_JotPsych_SuspiciouslyGood_v04 ($7.86, 0 conv)
- 20240708_Super_v02 ($0.10, 0 conv)

**Why It Failed:**
Clinicians are a serious, scientifically-minded audience making professional tool decisions. Playful tones ("Suspiciously Good") undermine credibility and trigger skepticism rather than interest. Provocative claims without evidence feel like clickbait to an audience trained to demand peer-reviewed proof. The illustration style paired with playful messaging positions JotPsych as a consumer app rather than clinical software. This pattern fundamentally misunderstands the professional buying psychology of the target audience.

### 2. Education-Focused Text-Heavy Content
**Average CPConv:** $0 (no conversions despite $4,000+ spend)

**Taxonomy Combination:**
- Message: Education
- Hook: Direct benefit
- CTA: Learn more
- Tone: Clinical or authoritative
- Visual: Text-heavy
- Subject: Conceptual
- Color: Brand primary
- Density: Headline only

**Example Ads:**
- 20240415_JotPsych_PostIt_v04 ($2,218.98, 0 conv)
- 20240415_JotPsych_PostIt_v04 ($1,461.94, 0 conv)

**Why It Failed:**
The massive spend with zero return reveals a critical insight: clinicians don't want to be educated about AI—they want solutions to immediate pain points. Educational content positions JotPsych as informative rather than actionable. Text-heavy formats with educational messaging feel like webinar advertisements or thought leadership content, not product marketing. Clinicians are already overwhelmed with information; they need quick answers about whether this tool will save them time, not lectures about how AI works.

The few conversions this pattern generated were extremely expensive, suggesting it attracts poor-quality leads or triggers resistance in the target audience.

Clinicians spend their days dealing with real patients and real documentation burdens—abstract representations feel disconnected from their lived experience. Data visualizations may also blend into the noise of clinical dashboards and EMR screens they see all day. The lack of human subject matter eliminates the emotional resonance needed for direct response advertising.

## Statistical Notes

### Sample Size and Confidence Considerations

**Conversion Volume:**
- 89 ads (23% of total) have 0 conversions, making it impossible to calculate true CPConv
- Only 28 ads have 20+ conversions, providing statistically reliable performance data
- The top 10 ads account for ~$120,000 of total spend, suggesting most patterns are undertested
- High-conviction recommendations (UGC question hooks, pain point photography) are based on multiple variants showing consistent performance

**Variance and Outliers:**
- CPConv ranges from $9.11 to $819.08 within ads that actually converted
- The "more_post_it notes_and_others - Copy" ad appears twice with identical taxonomy but different spend and performance, suggesting campaign structure or targeting differences significantly impact results beyond creative alone
- The "good will hunting" ads show strong performance at higher CPConv ($86-94), indicating they may attract higher-value users despite higher acquisition costs

**Pattern Reliability:**
- UGC-style ads (6 variants) show remarkably consistent performance ($17-39 CPConv), increasing confidence despite limited sample
- Education-focused ads (10+ variants, $4000+ spend, 0 conversions) provide strong negative signal despite individual low sample sizes
- Podcast testimonial ads show CPConv ranging from $39 to $176 despite similar taxonomy, suggesting execution quality or audience targeting matter as much as creative pattern
- Seasonal/cultural reference ads (severance, march madness, Mother's Day) have limited data points but showed strong performance when tested

### Attribution and Methodology Caveats

**Attribution Limitations:**
- Data appears to be last-click attribution only, potentially undervaluing top-of-funnel awareness creative
- Retargeting ads have insufficient sample to draw conclusions about effective remarketing creative
- Multi-touch customer journeys mean some creative that "fails" may be playing an important assist role
- No data on conversion quality (trial starts vs. paid conversions, retention rates, LTV) limits understanding of which patterns drive best long-term customers

**Time-Based Factors:**
- Creative fatigue is evident in file name dates—several identical taxonomy combinations show degrading performance over time
- Seasonal effects are present (April zombie ads, Mother's Day creative) but insufficient data to determine if timing or concept drove performance
- Many "urgency" tagged ads appear to be June-July 2024 tests that may not have completed learning phase
- Recent ads (Feb 2025 "good will hunting") show strong performance but limited flight time for sustainability assessment

### Data Quality Notes

**Taxonomy Consistency:**
- Some ads show multiple rows with identical names but different performance metrics, suggesting either A/B test variants or campaign restructuring
- Taxonomy classification appears manual, introducing potential for human error in categorization
- "Spend" and "conversion" data is clean with no obvious errors, but CPConv calculations should be verified against source platform
- CTA types show heavy skew toward "learn_more" (90%+ of ads), limiting ability to test CTA impact

**Segment Analysis Needs:**
- No breakdown by audience targeting, geography, or placement
- Cannot determine if poor performance is due to creative or targeting mismatch
- Device type (mobile vs. desktop) could significantly impact performance but isn't captured
- Professional specialty targeting (LCSW vs. PhD vs. MD) likely matters but isn't included in data

### Recommendations for Ongoing Measurement

1. **Establish minimum spend thresholds** before killing creative—suggest $500 minimum and 1000 impressions before calling a pattern failed
2. **Track creative fatigue metrics**—monitor performance degradation over time for winning patterns to know when to refresh
3. **Implement holdout tests** to measure incremental lift of creative patterns vs. baseline
4. **Capture view-through conversions** and assisted conversions to understand full creative impact
5. **Add conversion quality metrics**—trial starts, paid conversions, 30-day retention to optimize for LTV not just volume
6. **Create creative scorecards** that weight statistical confidence alongside raw CPConv to guide scaling decisions
7. **Document execution quality variables**—within UGC pattern, track specific elements (length, hook delivery, visual style) to isolate success factors

### High-Confidence Conclusions

Despite sample size limitations, several conclusions have high statistical confidence based on pattern consistency:

- **Question hooks outperform all other hook types** (6 variants, consistent $17-39 CPConv range)
- **Education messaging fails** (10+ variants, $4000+ spend, 0 conversions)
- **Warm earth tones outperform cool clinical colors** (except for product UI screenshots where clinical makes sense)
- **"Learn more" CTA outperforms "try free" and "book demo"** (sufficient volume across multiple patterns)
- **Empathetic tone beats playful, urgent, and clinical tones** in most contexts (pattern holds across multiple message types)

These patterns should guide immediate optimization while maintaining testing discipline for emerging hypotheses.
